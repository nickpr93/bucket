Using Machine Learning to Choose NBA Lineups
Disrupting Daily Fantasy

The Concept

Millions of Americans are now participating in daily fantasy sports, a previously unimagined 2.6 billion dollar endpoint for an industry that relied on casual office pools topping out at $500 bucks. The concept is simple: instead of working with a fixed lineup drafted at the beginning of the year, daily fantasy sites like DraftKings and FanDuel allow you to pick a team of players to compete on any given night. Instead of a one-time payment of $20 dollars, you can enter daily contests for as low as $1. Instead of your friends, you play against thousands, with a chance to win thousands more.


The rising popularity of daily fantasy sports, abetted by incessant ads on every major broadcaster, called into question the legality of the endeavor. After all, putting money against the performance of certain players sounds a bit like gambling. Heck, it sounds a lot like gambling.


Our goal is to determine whether fantasy sports are indeed games of chance. Using our knowledge of data science, the NBA, and a deep well of statistics dating back to 1985, we will attempt to pick the optimal lineup for DraftKings and FanDuel on any given night.

About Us


We are a group of graduate and undergraduate students at Harvard University working with some knowledge of statistics and an undying love for the Los Angeles Lakers. We believe that there is a way to look at past data and harness it for future knowledge. This time, we're choosing to use it on a game we're passionate for.



The Process


Although there is no direct model upon which we have built, there are many stories of FanDuel/DraftKings players who use their knowledge of statistics to predict the outcome of games. Some news outlets report that this 1.5 percent of fantasy players obtain 80 percent of the winnings. We are under the impression that most of their models involve simple Excel manipulation, not intense uses of analytics and data science. We have certainly considered their paths, but we look to create a more nuanced model.


Additionally, we have looked at the work of Bradley Effron and Carl Morris of Stanford University in conjunction with our the work of Nate Silver to perfect our "FabMelo" model. FabMelo, modeled after Silver's CARMELO, gets a particular player's closest historical relatives. We use that list to improve our estimates for the day's games, with the help of Stein's Paradox in Statistics.
